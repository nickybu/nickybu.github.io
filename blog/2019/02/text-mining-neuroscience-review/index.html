Text Mining and Neuroscience

The state-of-the-art, barriers and challenges.

University
 Fri, Feb 15, 2019 13 min read Neuroscience Text mining

An assignment submitted to The University of Manchester for COMP 61332 Text Mining

Text mining is an essential approach to uncovering neuroscience-related knowledge about the complex nature of the brain from large amounts of research data. Neuroscience encompasses the sciences that deal with the structure or function of the brain and nervous system. It incorporates fragments of a broad range of subdomains, including, but not limited to, molecular biology, anatomy, genetics and computer science. Retrieving, extracting and mining information from available research and literature data across the subdomains has proven to be a major challenge. Although text mining has been used considerably in bioinformatics, its adoption in the domain of neuroscience has been more recent. This paper examines the fundamental barriers and challenges in text mining for neuroscience and some current state-of-the-art resources.

Neuroinformatics aims to improve research capabilities by advancing data storage and organisation techniques, retrieval and analysis of the extensive amount of neuroscientific data. The existence of reliable neuroinformatics frameworks reduces the duration between the conception of a research project and its publication, along with increased potential for new discoveries. Numerous organisations have been crucial to the advancements in text mining for neuroscience over the past two decades, namely the Human Brain Project (HBP) [16], which was succeeded by the National Institutes of Health (NIH) [5], as well as the Society for Neuroscience (SfN) [17] and International Neuroinformatics Coordinating Facility (INCF) [2]. Organisations like these have conceived many projects contributing to a well-structured and thorough knowledge base for neuroscience data; some of the most prominent ones will be discussed further on.

Text mining in neuroscience has faced countless challenges, many of which not yet sufficiently solved. A common problem faced by all domains is the accessibility, or lack thereof, to the full-text of articles and research/experimental data. There are a number of journals which store articles behind a paywall, and most search engines do not allow the full-text of a paper to be searched, limiting access to their abstracts that often do not contain all the relevant information [13]. Data formats also affect accessibility, with considerable amounts of data not being shared in a machine-readable format. However, if the full-text is available then there exists a different challenge - an increased number of redundancies and false positives when querying, due to the large amount of information. Text mining offers increased levels of automation when it comes to reviewing corpora of literature, ranging from i) co-occurrence-based methods locating concepts occurring in the same region of text and postulating a relationship between them, ii) machine learning methods building classifiers based on statistic properties of the literature and iii) rule-based systems typically using manually-curated rule sets [4]. Textpresso for Neuroscience [13] and NeuroText [8] are two text mining tools for processing full-text and abstracts from neuroscience papers respectively.

Textpresso for Neuroscience stems from the original Textpresso text mining system, which supports annotating full-text biomedical papers and integrates with curation systems. Textpresso for Neuroscience forms part of the Neuroscience Information Framework created by the NIH Blueprint, with the intent of making neuroscience resources accessible and easily searchable. Its corpus spans 18 journals having articles available on PubMed [13] and contains 101,628 full-text articles, 232,515 abstracts and 255,344 titles as of February 2011. Documents are marked up with a number of categories and the use of ontologies improves the accuracy of specific searches within regions of the brain. Unfortunately, the corpus has not been updated since then, which seems to be a common issue across many digital resources. Oftentimes, grant funding is given for new projects rather than for maintaining existing resources beyond their initial funding period [3]. This results in initially powerful systems quickly becoming outdated due to the increasing quantity of literature.

NeuroText’s corpus is built from the Journal of Neuroscience and is used to populate the SenseLab [7] databases. NeuroText benefits from being continually updated by automatically downloading abstracts from the Journal of Neuroscience and is designed to be extended to various domains, in which case it would simply require a separate knowledge base [8]. Over the years, a large collection of databases and database suites have been curated for neuroscience. SenseLab was founded in 1993 as part of the HBP to publicise neural system literature, specifically neuronal membrance properties [7]. It contains several databases implemented using a unifying architecture that simplifies data creation and amendment. NeuronDB, CellPropDB and BrainPharm are some of the larger databases included. NeuroText facilitates the maintenance of such databases by aiding the database managers in categorising articles from their abstracts. NeuroExtract [9] complements NeuroText by supporting information retrieval and extraction from alternate sources by using concepts stored in NeuronDB and CellPropDB [7]. The lack of standardised neuroscience ontologies and vocabularies means that databases often adopt their own, thus making it harder to amalgamate databases and centralise neuroscience tools. In turn, curators spend more time building individual ontologies and end users will likely have inconsistent experiences across resources. This is one of the many barriers to integrating data across many sources, requiring considerable manual effort, often by experts in the field. Organisations and researchers alike have attempted to create standards for text mining neuroscienc- tific data. Significant contributions include The Neuroscience Information Framework (NIF) [11], which is a web portal for accessing neuroscience resources created by the NIH Blueprint. The NIF has created a comprehensive lexicon and ontology for neuroscience using open-source ontologies, currently utilising 265 sources. In the white paper for the NIF, Gardner et al. state that “Our Neuroscience Information Framework is not, cannot be, a complete solution. It is, however, an essential first step towards an integrated ecosystem for neuroscience.” [11]. This emphasises the complexity of the challenges involved in information retrieval, extraction and analysis in neuroscience.

The NIF makes use of NeuroLex [12], a dynamic lexicon of neuroscience concepts and terminology built using the Semantic MediaWiki platform. This allows neuroscientists to integrate their data into the NIF by submitting schema information and database views, keeping the data in its original format and maintaining data integrity [3]. NeuroLex handles mapping the data to a common ontology, providing meaningful querying opportunities across an extensive number of sources. NeuroLex is capable of assigning unique identifiers to resources, making it simpler to distinguish between concepts that share the same label. For example, the term ’nucleus’ can refer to part of a cell or part of the brain, and having a unique identifier for which part of the anatomy is being searched for is a powerful feature [1]. The primary value of NeuroLex is found in its organisation of anatonomical features and neuroscience concepts into a large data model with external references [12]. Furthermore, NeuroLex promotes collaborative research by allowing neuroscientists and ontologists to review anatomical features and concepts of neuroscience, link them to resources, form a general understanding with other neuroscientists and bring to light missing or incomplete concepts. Likewise, the rise of the World Wide Web enabled further collaborative efforts ranging from data availability and ease of access to data integration and semantic web applications.

The NIF standard ontology (NIFSTD) is a “semantically enhanced portal for accessing and integrating neuroscience data, tools and information” [14]. One of the central motivations is to avoid unnecessary efforts by conforming to existing ontologies and vocabularies. NeuroLex acts as the user interface to the NIFSTD. The ability to filter searches and infer useful classification through specifically defined concepts, such as classifying neurons by soma location or their morphology, is a compelling feature of the NIFSTD. The NIFSTD stems from the Biomedical Informatics Resource Network (BIRN) project lexicon (BIRNLex) ontology that largely extracted concepts from the Unified Medical Language System (UMLS) and NeuroNames [6]. UMLS is an ontology of biomedical concepts translating between terminology systems, while NeuroNames is a system of terminology developed as an attempt to standardise neuroanatomical terminology. NeuroNames contains more than 16,000 terms and 2,500 entities and provides acronyms, synonyms and homonyms. It is one of the three knowledge bases provided by BrainInfo, which is a web portal to neuroanatomical information. Brain Markup Language (BrainML) is another attempt at creating a standard for neuroscience data, this time focused on schema structure [10]. BrainML is being used to develop the NIF and defines a set of standards for using XML to facilitate information exchange. It is extremely difficult for a single standard to please all the people in the neuroscience community, as it is in most communities. Nonetheless, such endeavours at setting a de facto standard have facilitated neuroscientists with the ability to allocate more time towards discovery rather than towards data generation and curation.

The exponential growth in available data has caused remarkable advancements in machine learning, specifically deep learning, which has recently begun being explored in relation to text mining in neuroscience. Neuroimaging is an area that has heavily benefited from the advancements in image classification using deep learning. A recent work [15] looks at using deep learning models for named entity recognition (NER) on two computational neuroscience corpora. The primary goal of this work was to develop an approach to designing a NER tool that may be extended for many applications within neuroscience. A machine learning technique known as active learning was used to achieve this goal with manually-annotated corpora of small sizes. Active learning is a methodology capable of greatly reducing the number of annotated data needed for the training phase by using selection criteria to extract the most relevant sentences. Therefore, it can significantly reduce the amount of labelled data that is needed and the experts required to accurately label them, which significantly reduces the time and costs for manually annotating corpora. This deep learning NER tool performed very competitively with existing NER tools that require much larger corpora. This shows that when using active learning, performance is not directly correlated to corpus size. The major limitation discussed in the paper is the need for high quality annotations from field experts, which requires a considerable amount of time and expenses. Additionally, recognising terminology variation across papers still needs plenty of work.

Substantial amounts of research and studies have been carried out in the area of text mining and neuroscience, and the current state-of-the-art demonstrates how much work is still left to be done. This paper looked at some state-of-the-art resources that have had significant impacts on text mining in neuroscience, though there are a myriad of other resources and workflows being used. Text mining and natural language processing are currently popular areas in university courses and research, meaning that the current generation of students and researchers will be more educated when it comes to the available resources and techniques, as well as having more to work with. Nowadays, more and more individuals outside of computer science are familiarising themselves with text mining techniques. This may result in neuroscience and biology experts acquiring the necessary skills to perform text mining tasks without the assistance of a computer scientist, potentially producing higher quality resources at a faster rate.

It is crucial that organisations and neuroscientists continue to carry out research and make use of industry standard ontologies, terminologies and data formats. I believe the open source movement has already began to affect literature in neuroscience and will continue to do so, thus making both textual and image data more readily available to mine. The recent achievements in text mining exhibit a trend of automatic information retrieval, meaning that as time passes, the existing ontologies and neuroscience resources should be periodically or continuously updated with new or corrected information. The shift from exclusive manual input to a collaborative combination of manual and automated work will enable researchers to spend less time manually annotating data, instead focusing on exploring that data with the aid of machine learning techniques to uncover previously unknown information. Due to the nature of neuroscience spanning across so many subdomains, I hold the opinion that ideally experts should be working on domain-specific tools which may then be queried from a single interface, which corresponds to the trends being observed. Furthermore, I strongly believe that an avenue for further investigation is the application of deep learning techniques to images to establish concepts and relationships that textual data is incapable of. That being said, I feel that we are a while away from achieving full automation for text mining in neuroscience due to its complex nature and dependencies on expert knowledge, especially in identifying ambiguities.
